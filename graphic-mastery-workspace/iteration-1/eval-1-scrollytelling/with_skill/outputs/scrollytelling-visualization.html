Global Economic Development

Understanding how countries develop economically and what that means for their populations is crucial to addressing global inequality.

This visualization explores the relationship between a nation's gross domestic product (GDP) per capita—a measure of economic output per person—and life expectancy, a key indicator of living standards and healthcare quality.

Scroll down to explore three different perspectives on how the world's economies are distributed. The map below shows current GDP per capita across regions, with larger and darker circles indicating wealthier nations.

The Preston Curve: GDP vs Life Expectancy

As we shift our view, we see a striking pattern: there's a strong relationship between a country's wealth and how long its citizens live on average. This phenomenon, discovered by economist Samuel Preston, shows that wealthier nations consistently have higher life expectancies.

The relationship is strongest at lower income levels—as countries develop from poverty to middle income, life expectancy increases rapidly. The curve flattens at higher incomes, suggesting that wealth matters less for health once basic needs are met.

Different colors represent different world regions. Outliers—countries that defy the general trend—often have strong healthcare systems or face unique challenges.

Regional Inequality and Income Distribution

Finally, we see how the global population is distributed across income levels by region. This stacked view reveals critical disparities: some regions have concentrated wealth at the top, while others show more balanced distribution.

This perspective highlights why global development policy must be regional—solutions that work in one context may not work in another. Understanding these patterns helps policymakers target interventions where they'll have the most impact.

The challenge ahead: ensuring that economic growth translates into improved living standards for all, not just the wealthy.
