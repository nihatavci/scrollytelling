# The Last Telephone Operators: Voices From the Switchboard

This story is built around a series of recorded interviews — an oral history of the women who worked the manual telephone switchboards before automation erased the job.

In a recording made in 1989, Margaret Doyle, who started as an operator in 1948, describes her first day: "They sat me down in front of this wall of holes and cords, and within an hour I was connecting a doctor to a farm forty miles away. You held the whole town's voice in your hands."

The audio archive runs to more than sixty hours of taped conversation, collected by a regional museum between 1985 and 1992. In one clip, an operator recalls listening — against the rules — as news of the moon landing passed through her board. In another, a supervisor describes the night the lines flooded during a storm, every cord plugged, every operator talking at once.

What the recordings preserve is not just facts but voices: the cadence, the laughter, the long pauses. To hear Margaret Doyle describe "the click of a hundred cords" is to understand the job in a way no transcript can capture. The interviews were digitized in 2014 and now form the backbone of a listening exhibit — a podcast-style sequence where each voice introduces the next.

The switchboard fell silent in most towns by the late 1970s. But on tape, the operators are still connecting calls.
