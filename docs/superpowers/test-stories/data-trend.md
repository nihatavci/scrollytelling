# The Collapse: How the Cost of Reading a Human Genome Fell Off a Cliff

In 2001, sequencing a single human genome cost about 100 million dollars. It was a feat of national scale — the Human Genome Project had taken more than a decade and the combined effort of laboratories across the world.

By 2004, the cost had fallen to roughly 20 million dollars. Still astronomical, but the curve had begun to bend. In 2006 it dropped again, to around 10 million dollars per genome.

Then came the inflection. In 2008, a new generation of sequencing machines arrived, and the price cratered to about 1 million dollars. Just one year later, in 2009, it halved again to roughly 100 thousand dollars — a hundred-fold collapse from the start of the decade.

The fall accelerated. By 2012 a genome could be read for about 10 thousand dollars. By 2015, the figure was near 1,500 dollars. And by 2022, sequencing a human genome cost approximately 600 dollars — cheaper than many routine medical scans.

In two decades the price had fallen from 100 million dollars to 600 dollars: a drop of more than 99.999 percent. No technology in the history of medicine had ever become cheap so fast — far outpacing even Moore's Law, the famous rule that computing power doubles roughly every two years. The collapse of the genome's price did not just change biology. It quietly rewired what medicine could even imagine doing.
