# The Antikythera Mechanism: A Computer From the Ancient World

In 1901, sponge divers off the Greek island of Antikythera hauled up a corroded lump of bronze and wood from a Roman-era shipwreck. For decades it sat largely ignored in a museum drawer. Only later did researchers grasp what they were holding: the oldest known analog computer, built around 100 BCE.

The object itself is small — the largest surviving fragment is roughly the size of a hardback book. But inside that shoebox of bronze lay at least thirty interlocking gears, cut by hand to a precision that would not be matched in Europe for another fourteen centuries.

Turn the crank on its side, and the mechanism comes alive. The front face carries two concentric dials: one tracking the Egyptian calendar of 365 days, the other the twelve signs of the zodiac. A pointer shows the position of the sun against the stars; another, more elaborate, models the motion of the moon — including, astonishingly, the slight speeding-up and slowing-down of its orbit, reproduced through a clever pin-and-slot gear.

Flip to the back, and two great spiral dials dominate. The upper spiral is a 19-year calendar that reconciles the lunar months with the solar year. The lower spiral predicts eclipses, decades in advance, marking each with a glyph for solar or lunar, day or night.

What makes the Antikythera Mechanism extraordinary is not any single dial but the whole: a physical model of the cosmos, turned by hand, that compressed centuries of Babylonian and Greek astronomy into a device you could hold. To look at it from every angle — front dials, back spirals, the dense gearwork between — is to watch the ancient world think.
