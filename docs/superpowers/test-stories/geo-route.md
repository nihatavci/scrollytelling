# The Orient Express: A Journey Across a Continent

When the Orient Express made its inaugural run in October 1883, it stitched together a continent that had never before been crossed in such luxury and speed. The train departed from Paris, France — its grand terminus at the Gare de l'Est — and aimed for the edge of Europe itself: Constantinople, today's Istanbul, Turkey.

The first leg carried passengers east from Paris to Strasbourg, France, a distance of roughly 490 kilometres, hugging the valleys that lead toward the Rhine. Crossing into the German lands, the train pressed on to Munich, the Bavarian capital, where the Alps first loomed on the southern horizon.

From Munich the route turned southeast toward Vienna, Austria — the imperial heart of the Habsburg empire and, for many travellers, the true beginning of "the East." Vienna to Budapest, Hungary, was a short and celebrated stretch along the Danube, just 240 kilometres of riverbank and farmland.

Budapest marked the threshold of the Balkans. The line ran on to Bucharest, Romania, some 640 kilometres further, through the Carpathian foothills. In the early years passengers transferred to a steamer for part of the journey, but by 1889 a continuous rail route reached all the way to the Bosphorus.

The final approach delivered travellers to Constantinople — Istanbul — on the shores where Europe meets Asia, more than 3,000 kilometres from the Paris platform where they had begun. The whole journey took about 80 hours.

What made the Orient Express extraordinary was not merely the distance but the idea: that Paris, Vienna, Budapest, Bucharest and Istanbul could be threaded onto a single ribbon of steel, each city a scene in one continuous story of arrival and departure. For more than a century the names of those stations carried the romance of the journey between them.
